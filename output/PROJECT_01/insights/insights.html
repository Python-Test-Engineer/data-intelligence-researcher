Executive Summary

This sales order dataset ($97,658 revenue, 48 clean orders, Jan–May 2025) reveals a business with balanced geography and a diversified product line but with two critical structural weaknesses: zero customer retention (every order is from a unique buyer) and hidden regional product gaps (Consulting is absent from East and West). Revenue peaked in March before declining through May, with an April volume-revenue divergence signalling shrinking deal sizes. The discount structure is tiered and controlled, but one rep (Priya Sharma) gives significantly higher average discounts. Addressing the retention gap and unlocking Consulting in underserved regions represent the two highest-leverage growth opportunities.

Key Insights

1

Zero Customer Retention Is the Single Biggest Strategic Risk

Source: top_customers.png, order_status.png, cumulative_revenue.png

All 48 clean orders come from 48 unique customers — not a single repeat buyer in 5 months. The top 10 customers account for only 34.8% of revenue, far below typical B2B Pareto concentration. Without recurring revenue, the business must acquire a new customer for every sale, making revenue inherently unpredictable and acquisition-cost dependent.

Implication: Building a customer success function and launching win-back campaigns for top customers should be the number one strategic priority.

High Confidence Zero-repeat finding is unambiguous across the entire dataset.

2

Consulting Revenue Absent from Half the Country

Source: revenue_heatmap.png, revenue_by_category.png, revenue_by_region.png

The heatmap reveals two $0 cells: East × Consulting and West × Consulting. Consulting has the highest AOV ($2,503) of any category but generates revenue only in North ($9,675) and South ($10,350). Even 3–4 Consulting deals per region at the existing AOV would add $7,500–$10,000 in revenue.

Implication: Expanding Consulting into East and West is a high-AOV, low-volume growth lever — 8–10% revenue uplift with no new product development required.

High Confidence Zero-revenue cells are definitive; AOV advantage is validated.

3

March Peak Followed by Revenue Decline Signals a Q2 Problem

Source: monthly_revenue.png, cumulative_revenue.png

Revenue peaked at ~$25,000 in March (+46% MoM), then declined in April (-8.7%) and collapsed in May (-37.4%). April’s decline is notable because order count was at its highest (12 orders) — meaning the drop was driven by smaller deals, not fewer deals.

Implication: The April volume-up / revenue-down pattern suggests deal quality erosion. If May’s decline is structural, the business faces a Q2 revenue trajectory problem requiring immediate pipeline review.

Medium Confidence May is likely a partial month, limiting the decline’s true magnitude.

4

Tiered Discount Structure Is Sound, but One Rep Is an Outlier

Source: discount_distribution.png, sales_rep_performance.png

Discounts cluster at discrete tiers (0%, 5%, 10%, 15%, 20%) with 60–65% of orders at 0–5%. Priya Sharma averages 8.6% discount — nearly double some colleagues — yet her revenue ($22,092) is the lowest of the four named reps.

Implication: Sharma’s higher discount rate without proportionally higher volume suggests discounting is eroding margin. A coaching intervention or discount approval workflow for deals above 10% may be warranted.

High Confidence Discrete discount values and rep patterns are clear.

5

Product Revenue Is Driven by Pricing Power, Not Volume

Source: revenue_by_product.png, revenue_by_category.png

All six products have exactly 8 orders each. Revenue differences ($13,725–$20,025) are entirely explained by unit price and discount variation. Data Cleansing Service leads at $20,025 ($2,503 AOV) while AI Training Workshop trails at $13,725 ($1,716 AOV) — a 46% gap on equal volume.

Implication: The revenue leaderboard is a pricing power ranking. AI Training Workshop may be underpriced; a $200 per-deal price increase would add $1,600 to total revenue with no volume change needed.

High Confidence 8-orders-per-product uniformity is definitive.

6

Regional Balance Masks Radically Different Category Mixes

Source: revenue_by_region.png, revenue_heatmap.png

The regional bar chart shows a tight $22K–$27K spread, suggesting homogeneity. But the heatmap reveals entirely different compositions: South is Consulting + Web Dev heavy; West is Software-dominant with zero Consulting; North is balanced; South’s Training is almost zero ($855).

Implication: One-size-fits-all regional strategy would be ineffective. Marketing and rep training should be localised by category strength.

High Confidence Annotated heatmap values leave no ambiguity.

7

The “(unknown)” Rep Order Is a Data Governance Red Flag

Source: sales_rep_performance.png

One order ($3,000) has no sales rep attribution. While small in absolute terms, if this gap scales with volume (2% rate), it would distort commissions, territory metrics, and pipeline forecasting.

Implication: Investigate and fix the CRM gap at the source — manual entry error, integration failure, or departed rep.

High Confidence Single data point, but governance implication is systemic.

Patterns Across Analyses

  1. Surface uniformity conceals structural imbalance: Region, product, and rep charts all show near-parity at the aggregate level. But the heatmap, discount patterns, and customer data reveal hidden asymmetries — zero Consulting in two regions, zero repeat customers, one rep discounting more aggressively.
  2. Volume is uniform; value is the differentiator: With 8 orders per product and 11–13 per rep, all revenue variation comes from pricing, discounting, and product mix — not volume. Growth strategy should focus on value per deal.
  3. March is the pivot month: Multiple charts converge on March as the inflection point — peak monthly revenue, steepest cumulative gradient, and the month after which both metrics decline.
  4. Data quality is good but has edges: Two dirty rows removed, one mixed-format date, one missing rep, one missing email. These suggest the CRM has no hard validation at the point of capture.

Recommended Next Steps

High Priority

  1. Customer Retention Programme — Design post-purchase follow-up and re-engagement. Even 10% retention adds ~$10K/month.
  2. Consulting Expansion Pilot — Assign Consulting sales resource in East and West. Target 3–5 deals per region in Q3.
  3. May Data Verification — Confirm whether May is complete or partial. Adjust all trend analyses.

Medium Priority

  1. Discount Governance Review — Audit Sharma’s patterns; implement approval step for >10% discounts.
  2. March Decomposition — Break down March peak by product, region, rep to find repeatable drivers.
  3. Fix Unknown Rep — Resolve the unattributed $3,000 order’s CRM gap.

Exploratory

  1. Training Repositioning — Test bundling AI Training with Software/Web Dev to lift AOV.
  2. Cohort Analysis — As data grows, build acquisition cohorts to measure retention signals.
  3. Regional Pricing Test — Test Consulting offers in East/West to validate demand.
